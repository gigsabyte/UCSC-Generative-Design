Final Project Proposal: Purgatory Court

Artist Statement

Purgatory Court is an attempt to create a generator for Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney trials. In Phoenix Wright, the player has to solve the mystery of who committed a murder while acting as the defense lawyer of a falsely accused innocent. This is accomplished by collecting evidence and using it to point out contradictions in witnesses’ testimony during a murder trial. Every trial is essentially a hand-written murder-mystery story made interactive through witness cross-examination. Having already learned how to create story variations using nondeterministic generative grammars, I’d like to see if I can break Phoenix Wright cases down to their bare essentials and make those essentials modular. The result will hopefully be an experience that somewhat replicates the feeling of a playing Phoenix Wright game, except forever.

I decided to take Generative Design because I wanted to challenge my own biases that favor hand-written/drawn/constructed media over generated media. I have to admit that I still do prefer the former, but I have grown to see the value in the latter. By making Purgatory Court, I hope to combine one of the most linear, deliberately constructed games I know with a method of making content open-ended.

The Experience

Purgatory Court is a replication of the “trial” part of a Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney case. It begins in the courtroom, with the player character seemingly waking up in the middle of a trial. The player will have a list of evidence available to them, with each piece of evidence given a generated description. After being given a brief description of the case by the judge, a witness will begin providing testimony. The player must then find contradictions in the testimony and point them out with evidence. If the player succeeds, they will be taken to the next testimony, but if they fail, they will lose “health.” The game ends when the player makes too many mistakes and loses all their “health,” but if the player points out all the contradictions and solves the case, they find themselves back at the beginning of another newly generated trial.

The visual/audio style is whatever I have time to implement, since the generation is mostly text-based. I’ll most likely be able to draw some art, and will find appropriate royalty-free music.

Technical Details

I will use nondeterministic generative grammars to generate trials. Essentially, each trial will be based on a false narrative axiom such as “<victim> was hit with <weapon> at <time>. <witness> happened to be <action> nearby and saw the incident. <client> was found nearby with <evidence>,” and a true narrative axiom such as “<culprit> hit <victim> with <weapon> before placing <evidence> on <client>.”

The trial begins with a presentation of the generated false narrative. Witness testimony will also be generated using different axioms. There will be two to three witnesses that each have their own testimony axiom: one to prove the <client> wasn’t the murderer, one to prove the <culprit> as the scene (thus calling in the <culprit> as the <witness>), and one where the <culprit> implicates themself. Each piece of <evidence> will have a list of traits, and testimony will be generated to have at least one <contradiction> where the witness describes the <evidence> incorrectly. When the player succeeds at pointing out the last <contradiction>, the <culprit> will reveal the true generated narrative.

All of this will be implemented in Unity, using some prior code I have written for a visual novel system.

Stretch goal: organize contradictions by difficulty and modify the probability of an easy/medium/hard contradiction happening based on the time it took the player to find the last contradiction.
